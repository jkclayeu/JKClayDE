Every piece begins as a shapeless lump, and the first job is to remove its air. Wedging is a rhythmic kneading that pushes out bubbles which would otherwise crack a piece in the kiln. It is quiet, physical work, and a good warm-up for the hands.

Throwing is the part everyone pictures: clay rising between wet fingers on a spinning wheel. But the form that comes off the wheel is only half-made. After a day of drying it goes back on to be trimmed — the foot carved, the walls refined, the weight balanced.

Then comes waiting. A bisque firing hardens the raw clay; glazing dresses it in colour; and a second, hotter firing at cone 6 melts that glaze into glass. From lump to shelf is the better part of a week, most of it spent patiently waiting for the kiln.
